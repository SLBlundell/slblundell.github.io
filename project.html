The Midas Touch:
What is the on-court impact of salary within the NBA?


During the 1997/98 NBA season, Michael Jordan earned $33,140,000 ($61 million adjusted for inflation) whilst playing in a Chicago Bulls jersey for the last time. Within the context of today's NBA, where 30 players will earn more than $33m this year, this sounds like a steal for the world’s most valuable athlete. However, this figure balloons to astronomical proportions when considering the fact that the entire NBA was worth 14x less than it is today. MJ’s salary was so disproportionately large compared to his peers that he was paid more than the combined payrolls of 295 players on 19 teams that season. The NBA infamously instituted a max salary cap the following season to prevent such occurrences in the future.

NBA Salaries within the context of world sports

Whilst the days of league transcending salaries for individual players may be over, the NBA is uniquely filled with highly paid stars compared to other sports. Even within the NBA, the salaries of the league’s premier stars outstrip the majority of players by considerable amounts.

With league revenues increasing at substantial rates, and a greater proportion of that revenue being spent on players, it is more important than ever for franchises to distribute their salaries effectively.

Motivating the question

This motivates the question: Are NBA franchises justified in distributing their limited salary cap amongst a few, highly paid stars?

In order to answer this, we must also determine what level of performance is correlated with salary, both on the franchise and individual player levels. Can franchises still perform in the playoffs without out-spending other teams? Through exploration of these questions, we explore two significant topics. Not only do we begin to uncover the level of fiscal efficiency within the NBA, but we also gain insight into how the composition of salary rules within the NBA effect outcomes on-the-court. These insights can, in theory, be applied within other sporting environments.

Payrolls, playoff performance and factors impacting team success

We must first consider what factors contribute the most to a team’s playoff performance, our chosen success metric. Our columns of interest are playoff wins (P_W) and team payroll (Pay). We can see that out of all measurable game statistics, overall shooting efficiency ratings (such as TS% and eFG%) and margin of victory indicators (SRS, MOV) are the best indicators of a team’s success in the playoffs. If teams are distributing their salary caps as efficiently as possible, we would expect these indicators to also be highly correlated with payroll. We can see, however, that team payrolls are only somewhat correlated with efficiency ratings, and extremely uncorrelated with both margin of victory indicators and playoff performance. In short, teams within the modern NBA that spend more do not win more.
